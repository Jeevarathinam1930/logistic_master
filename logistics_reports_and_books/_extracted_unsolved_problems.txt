Unsolved Logistics & Supply Chain Problems
Despite massive investment in software (ERP , TMS, WMS, control towers, IoT, AI, etc.), critical logistics
problems persist. Industry experts note that many companies have real-time visibility but still cannot
translate data into action, leaving a visibility–execution gap【10†L104-L113】【42†L386-L395】.
Fragmented systems (TMS, WMS, carrier portals, ERPs) mean no single source of truth, so even when delays
are detected, teams waste days reconciling conflicting data【42†L420-L429】【10†L147-L155】. In practice
this means missed deliveries, detention/demurrage fees, and unhappy customers despite having
dashboards. For example, a typical company might have 5–10 platforms each with its own “truth,” so a delay
seen in one system must be matched in others, costing days in response time【42†L420-L429】【10†L147-
L155】. 
Existing solutions include transportation management systems, visibility platforms, and “control towers”
that aggregate trackers and EDI feeds. But they often fail because siloed data, human workflows, and rigid
processes prevent automated responses. Gartner found only 19% of firms fully integrate scenario planning
into operations, so when disruptions occur the rest still “react” rather than “plan”【10†L147-L155】. In short,
visibility alone is not enough – companies still lack true end-to-end control. 
Severity: Very high (Frequency ~10, Impact ~9). Nearly all shippers and 3PLs are affected. Financial impact:
Every extra day of delay can incur high demurrage and customer penalties. AI/ML Opportunity: Strong (AI/
agents can recommend actions, multi-agent control towers). Start-up angle: Platforms that fuse multi-
system data and automate decision (market ripe, competition moderate). 
1. Incomplete Visibility & Execution Gap
Description: Companies have “awareness” of shipment status but lack the ability to act on it. For example, a
dashboard may show a truck is delayed, but teams must manually decide on re-routing or expedite – often
too late【10†L104-L113】【42†L386-L395】. This occurs across all modes (ocean, air , trucking). Shippers,
LSPs and 3PLs are most affected. Root causes: siloed data and systems, lack of integrated workflows, no
ownership of exceptions, and rigid processes【10†L147-L155】【42†L398-L404】. Existing solutions:
Freight visibility platforms, control tower services, TMS exceptions management. Why fail: They produce
alerts but don’t connect to decision engines or business rules. Control towers often stay “reactive” –
collecting data but not prescriptive. IBM/Siemens argue AI-driven control towers must be predictive and
prescriptive, but many firms have only basic dashboards【10†L147-L155】. Evidence: “Visibility is only
useful when it supports planning, decision-making, and execution” – without that, firms “see more” but can’t
“do more”【10†L147-L155】【42†L386-L395】.
Impact: Frequent (score ~9) and high (score ~9). Missed SLAs, demurrage, expedite charges.

customs). There is no “single source of truth,” so conflicting schedules or EDI data cause confusion. For
example, a TMS may say a shipment arrives Saturday while a WMS says Sunday, triggering costly re-plans

3. Last-Mile Delivery Complexity
Description: The final delivery step is unpredictable: urban congestion, customer availability, and single-
package drops. Costs skyrocket (often >50% of transport spend【12†L471-L480】) and customer
expectations (fast, free, reliable) strain operations. Root causes: Fragmented e-commerce demand (millions
of single deliveries)【12†L504-L513】, static routes that don’t adapt to real-time conditions【12†L522-
L530】, poor address data (especially in dense/ non-standard areas)【12†L534-L543】, and driver/customer
behavior . Existing solutions: Route optimization software, crowd-sourced drivers, local micro-hubs, locker
networks【15†L174-L182】. Why fail: Static planning ignores traffic and real-time events【12†L522-L530】.
Address/geolocation errors still cause repeated attempts【13†L1-L9】. Consumer expectations (free, same-
day) often outstrip economics. Tools exist but fragmentation and manual tasks persist.
Evidence: LogiNext notes last-mile often “breaks” despite upstream controls – non-intuitive factors (address
ambiguity, static routing) cause costly failures【12†L504-L513】【12†L522-L530】. DHL data: 53% of
shipping cost is in the last mile【15†L197-L204】.
Impact: Very high (score ~9). Millions lost in extra mileage, failed deliveries (customer churn), and overtime.
AI/Optimization: Dynamic routing with real-time traffic, predictive ETAs, autonomous vehicles have
promise but not widespread yet. Startup: High (market ~$150B by 2027【15†L205-L212】) but crowded
(Uber/Ryder/etc).

5. Empty Container Repositioning
Description: Global trade imbalances leave excess empties in some regions and shortages in others.
Moving empties costs an estimated >$20 billion/year【8†L174-L182】. For example, Europe & Americas
have surplus empties while Asia often needs more, causing carriers to pay to backhaul empties. Root
causes: Persistent trade imbalances and scheduling mismatches. Even with technology planning, empty
flows cannot be perfectly optimized – you can’t guarantee cargo on every return leg【8†L185-L193】. 
Existing solutions: Container-exchange platforms (xChange), pooling, shared ownership (SOC programs),
and planning tools. Why fail: Technology helps visibility but “cannot completely offset the structural challenges
of trade imbalances”【8†L207-L215】. Every operator shows that no matter how smart the routing, empty
moves are often inevitable. Ocean carriers add “empty repositioning” surcharges reflecting this cost.
Evidence: Crane Worldwide notes empty logistics is “often overlooked,” yet costs billions annually【8†L174-
L182】. It debunks myths: tech can help planning but not eliminate the imbalance【8†L207-L215】. Impact:
High (score ~7). Direct cost ($20B), plus port congestion from idle empties. AI: Forecasting demand-driven
reposition needs is possible, but structural (geography) limit win. Startup: Niche market (few players like
Container xChange), moderate competition; ROI depends on savings. 
6. Labor Shortages and Skills Gap
Description: Chronic shortages of drivers, warehouse workers, and supply-chain professionals plague
every logistics segment. In the U.S., 74% of transportation/logistics employers struggle to find skilled talent
【21†L164-L172】. Europe faces similar woes – over half of trucking firms report they cannot grow due to
lack of drivers【33†L42-L50】. As experienced Baby Boomers retire, young people often avoid physically
demanding logistics jobs【23†L137-L146】【21†L172-L180】. Root causes: Aging workforce, low wages/
long hours, pandemic-induced shifts away from “blue-collar” jobs, and immigration policy tightening
【23†L141-L150】【21†L172-L180】. Existing solutions: Automation (robots, AGVs), higher pay, training
programs, gig-economy models. Why fail: Automation is capital-intensive and not universally applicable
(many tasks still need humans). Incentives and training take years to show effect. Also, logistics cannot offer
remote work flexibility that other industries do.
Evidence: ManpowerGroup reports 74% of U.S. logistics companies have hiring trouble, citing aging drivers
and disinterest from youth【21†L164-L172】. SupplyChainBrain (May 2025) calls it a “persistent crisis” that
will “destabilize all aspects” of supply chains【23†L115-L124】 – already leading to warehouses running
below capacity and shipping delays【23†L121-L124】.
Impact: Very high (score ~9). Delays, overtime costs, increased freight rates. AI/Robotics: Autonomous
trucks and warehouse robots can help but are still nascent. Startup: Very high opportunity (labortech,
robots, training platforms) but also high competition.

7. Port Congestion and Terminal Delays
Description: Major ports (e.g. Los Angeles, Rotterdam, Singapore) regularly face vessel backlogs and yard
congestion, causing delivery delays and surcharges. Recent crises (Panama drought, Red Sea conflicts) have
exacerbated port congestion globally【8†L156-L164】【29†L63-L71】. Root causes: Surge in trade
volumes, labor shortages at docks【29†L119-L124】【29†L126-L134】, infrastructure limits, and
unpredictable events (strikes, weather , routing changes). Existing solutions: Port appointment systems,
slot booking, 24/7 operations, and now predictive AI (e.g. Portcast). Why fail: Even with scheduling, the
upstream chaos (late ships, customs holds) means slots are missed. AI can forecast port-level trends, but
localized terminal congestion still surprises operators. The Siemens/Portcast example: without AI, all one
sees is “Le Havre is congested” – with AI, they pinpoint exactly which terminal is blocked【44†L295-L304】.
Traditional port systems don’t provide that granularity.
Evidence: Sinay reports “port congestion has worsened” in 2024, raising shipping costs (higher spot rates,
demurrage surcharges) and driving inflation【29†L86-L95】. Global volume surges and labor gaps have
“strained port operations, causing significant delays that ripple through the supply chain”【29†L114-L124】
【29†L126-L134】. Delays force shippers to hold more inventory, increasing warehousing costs and risk of
spoilage【29†L98-L102】.
Impact: High (score ~8). Congested ports can idle thousands of TEUs, incurring demurrage ($/container/
day) and missed production schedules. AI/IoT: Promising (predictive port analytics), but adoption is early. 
Startup: Port analytics is growing (market in hundreds of millions), moderate competition. 
8. Customs & Trade Compliance Delays
Description: Complex, changing regulations (tariffs, trade wars, sanctions) make cross-border shipping
unpredictable. Shipments often get held up by missing/incomplete documentation or sudden tariff
changes. Root causes: Frequent regulatory changes (e.g. 72% of trade pros cite tariff volatility impacting
supply chain【27†L233-L242】), as well as legacy paperwork processes. Existing solutions: Global Trade
Management (GTM) software, HS code databases, compliance teams. Why fail: New rules appear faster
than companies can update systems. Manual checking still common (e.g. invoices, licenses). Automation
can flag some risks, but human review is still needed. Even big ERP suppliers warn that trade compliance is
only as good as the underlying data.
Evidence: Thomson Reuters (Feb 2026) reports “72% of trade professionals identified U.S. tariff volatility as
the most impactful regulatory change,” doubling year-over-year【27†L239-L248】. SupplyChainBrain notes
that documentation and customs clearance complexity now “impact project schedules,” and firms are
treating “customs delays” as enterprise risk【27†L295-L304】【27†L328-L337】. Failure to comply can result
in massive fines or held shipments.
Impact: Medium-high (score ~7). Delays and penalties can cost millions for large shippers. AI: NLP tools can
parse documents, ML can suggest HS codes, but any error is costly. Startup: Niche (compliance software),
but needs deep content expertise; competition from big GTM suites. 
9. Forecasting & Inventory Balancing
Description: Aligning supply and demand remains elusive. Over-forecasting leads to excess stock (capital
tied up), under-forecasting causes stockouts and lost sales【36†L115-L124】【36†L125-L134】. This spans
all industries (retail, CPG, manufacturing). Root causes: Volatile demand (COVID, promotions, seasonality),
limited historical data, siloed channels (omnichannel sales), and lack of demand signal from customers
down the chain. Existing solutions: Advanced planning systems (SAP IBP , Kinaxis), ML forecasting tools.

Why fail: Garbage in, garbage out: forecasts rely on incomplete/inaccurate data, and unexpected events
(pandemics, fashion trends) defy models. Multi-echelon inventory optimization exists but is hard to

Evidence: Industry blogs note that “inaccurate forecasting” causes stock imbalances【36†L184-L193】, and

10. Infrastructure Constraints
Description: Physical capacity (roads, rails, warehouses) is strained. In many regions, aging highways and
ports create unpredictable transit times. For example, European road congestion adds many hours of
delay on key lanes【33†L59-L63】. Warehouses also face space and equipment shortages (material
handling, storage). Root causes: Underinvestment, urbanization, and growing freight volumes. Existing
solutions: Public-private logistics hubs, intermodal shifts, warehouse automation. Why fail: Infrastructure
projects take decades and capital. Rapid e-commerce growth outpaces new capacity.
Evidence: TAWI notes that “current road congestion results in longer, unpredictable journey times,”
undermining competitiveness【33†L59-L63】. In warehouses, insufficient tools/machinery create
bottlenecks【33†L65-L68】.
Impact: Moderate (score ~6). Adds hidden lead-time buffer costs and uncertainty. AI/Optimization: Traffic/
flow analysis can route vehicles around congestion, but physical fixes are still needed. Startup: Focus on
optimizing existing capacity (e.g. dynamic scheduling), some niche opportunity. 
11. Sustainability & ESG Compliance
Description: Logistics contributes over one-third of global CO₂ emissions【33†L79-L85】. Regulators and
customers demand greener operations. Tracking and reducing carbon footprint across a multi-modal
network remains hard. Root causes: Heavy reliance on fossil fuels (trucks, ships, planes), and lack of
standardized data for emissions. Existing solutions: Telematics (fuel monitoring), route optimization,
alternative fuels (EV, hydrogen). Why fail: Green technologies (EV trucks, e-vans) are still expensive or
immature【15†L222-L231】. Supply chain carbon tracking is often manual or estimated, causing
compliance headaches. Packaging mandates (e.g. recyclable, lightweight) add new handling requirements
in warehouses【33†L79-L85】【33†L87-L90】.
Evidence: TAWI: “Logistics, which accounts for just over a third of global greenhouse gas emissions, is under
intense scrutiny”【33†L79-L85】. Xeneta reports container shipping emissions were +13.8% in Jan–Oct 2024
vs 2023, making 2050 net-zero goals very ambitious【34†L463-L472】.
Impact: Growing (score ~7). Non-compliance risks fines and loss of business. AI: Route/flow optimization
can reduce emissions; ML can model footprint. Startup: Cleantech in logistics (fleet management, carbon
accounting) is hot, but also competitive and capital-intensive.

time,” shippers incur steep demurrage/detention penalties (often $75–$300+/day per container【47†L487-

Problems That Current AI Solutions Still Cannot Reliably Solve
These are issues that even the most advanced AI/analytics and agent systems struggle with due to
incomplete data, unpredictability, or coordination requirements:
Terminal Congestion Prediction: AI can predict some port-level trends, but individual terminal
queues (and resulting demurrage) are still hard to forecast without proprietary port data【44†L295-
L304】.
Demurrage Prevention: Anticipating and preventing per-container fees requires flawless end-to-
end visibility and coordination among carriers, customs, drayage – a tall order despite AI alerts
【47†L514-L521】.
Container Availability Forecasting: Predicting where empties will be next-needed is inherently
uncertain, tied to global imbalances【8†L174-L182】.
Cross-Company Data Sharing: Enforcing data sharing across competitors (e.g. joint inventory
status) is as much a business/policy problem as a technical one.

AI and Startup Opportunities
For each problem above, new AI/ML approaches or multi-agent systems could help. For example:
AI-Powered Control Towers: Combining end-to-end data with machine learning to not just alert but 
orchestrate responses (shipment re-route, mode shifts, etc.)【44†L237-L245】【42†L398-L404】.
Autonomous Last-Mile Routing: Real-time optimization using on-ground sensors (traffic, parking)
and learning from historical patterns.
Cold-Chain Sensors & Analytics: IoT + AI anomaly detection can catch early signs of temperature
breach, enabling automated corrective action.
Smart Container Pools: Platforms that use AI to suggest repositioning trades between carriers/
shippers to reduce empties.
Talent Platforms: AI-driven workforce planning to predict and mitigate labor gaps; robotics/AR to
augment labor .
PortAI: Predictive terminal simulations that integrate vessel schedules, yard status and weather to
optimize berth planning.
Blockchain / Federated Data Networks: To enable cross-company visibility without exposing raw
data – yet still research-level in adoption.
Dynamic Contracting Tools: ML systems that automatically switch between contracted spot buying
to control costs.
AI/Predictive Analytics can partially address many issues (predicting delays, optimizing routing,
forecasting demand), but often fail without trustworthy, real-time data and clear processes for execution
【42†L398-L404】【10†L147-L155】. Multi-agent systems (like negotiated coordination between carrier-
agents, shipper-agents, port-agents) hold promise for distributed decision problems (e.g. balancing a fleet
across carriers). Reinforcement learning has seen interest for dynamic pricing and routing, but the
complexity and risk-aversion in logistics slow adoption.

Startup and Market Insights
LaborTech & Automation: With labor shortage scoring very high, solutions that enhance worker
productivity or recruit talent have billion-dollar potential. However , the field is competitive (people
search, gig platforms, robots).
Visibility Platforms: Many startups (and incumbents like Project44, FourKites) compete, but the
“execution gap” suggests a niche for smarter decision tools.
Supply Chain Control Towers: Gartner and others champion them, but only ~19% have effective
ones. Startups that can integrate data silos and apply AI prescriptively could capture value.
Cold-Chain Monitoring: The $100B+ market (projected by 2026【19†L145-L153】) still needs better
solutions. Startups with low-cost sensors or AI diagnostics can grow.
Sustainability Tracking: ESG compliance is mandatory for public firms (SEC rules), so startups that
automate carbon accounting and footprint visibility have room, though larger firms may build in-
house.
Empty Fleet Platforms: Few players exist (Container xChange etc.), so startups facilitating shared
logistics assets may find niche success.
Customs and Compliance Software: High barriers (regulation expertise needed) but large
incumbents (Thomson Reuters, Amber Road) dominate; smaller players could focus on machine-
learning classification to reduce manual customs checks.
Last-Mile Optimization: Enormous market (growing with e-commerce), but also dominated by
delivery platforms; startups must target specific inefficiencies (e.g. address validation, micro-
fulfillment).
Others: Blockchain startups see trouble gaining traction in logistics (per Reddit/industry). Startups
focused on unsolved problems (like multi-modal decisioning) might face high R&D costs and slow
sales cycles.
